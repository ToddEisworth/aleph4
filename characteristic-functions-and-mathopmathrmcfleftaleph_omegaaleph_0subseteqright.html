    Section 2 Characteristic Functions and \(\mathop{\mathrm{cf}}\left([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0},\subseteq\right)\)

    Where are we headed now? Our next stop is Shelah’s theorem that
    \begin{equation*} \max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}\left(\{\aleph_n:n<\omega\}\right) = \mathop{\mathrm{cf}}\left([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0},\subseteq\right), \end{equation*}
    a theorem that gets at the heart of the connection between traditional cardinal arithmetic and pcf theory.
    In this section, we modify our usual assumptions about \(A\) and its associated pcf structure by demanding
    \begin{equation*} |A|^+<\min(A) \end{equation*}
    (this is innocuous as far as pcf theory is concerned), and we assume \(\kappa\) is a regular cardinal such that
    \begin{equation*} |A|<\kappa=\mathop{\mathrm{cf}}\kappa<\min(A). \end{equation*}
    Our assumption on \(|A|\) is so that such a \(\kappa\) exists.
    For the remainder this section, we will let \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{,}\) \(B_\lambda[A]\) and \(\langle f^\lambda_\alpha:\alpha<\lambda\rangle\) be as in the preceding section, for each \(\lambda\in\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.1 Internally Approachable Models

    Our main tool in this section and the next is internally approachable models, which we have seen already in class during our investigation of \(I[\lambda]\text{.}\) Recall:
    Definition 6. We say that \(N\) (an elementary submodel of some expansion of \(\langle H(\chi),\in, <_\chi\rangle\) for some sufficiently large regular cardinal \(\chi\)) is \(\kappa\)-internally approachable if \(N\) is the union of a sequence \(\langle N_i:i<\kappa\rangle\)
    • \(\langle N_i:i<\kappa\rangle\) is a continuous \(\in\)-sequence of elementary submodels of \(H(\chi)\)
    • \(\displaystyle \langle N_j:j\leq i\rangle\in N_{i+1}\)
    • \(|N_i|<\kappa\) and \(N_i\cap\kappa\) is an initial segment of \(\kappa\text{.}\)
    We are going to want to assume that \(A\) (together with its associated pcf structure) is an element of \(N_0\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.2 Characteristic functions of models

    One of the reasons behind the usefulness of requiring \(|A|<\kappa<\min(A)\) is the observation that \(A\) is not only an element of \(N_0\text{,}\) but it is a subset of \(N_0\) as well because we assume \(N_0\cap\kappa\) is an initial segment of \(\kappa\text{.}\) Furthermore, for any \(a\in A\) we know
    \begin{equation*} \sup(N\cap a)<a, \end{equation*}
    and so \(N\) can be used to define a natural function in \(\prod A\text{,}\) the characteristic function of \(N\):
    Definition 7. The characteristic function \(\chi_N\) of \(N\) in \(\prod A\) is defined by
    \begin{equation*} \chi_N(a) = \sup(N\cap a) \end{equation*}
    We will also need to consider the characteristic functions of models \(N_i\) for \(i<\kappa\text{.}\) These functions are defined in the obvious way, and note that for each \(i<\kappa\text{,}\) the sequence \(\langle \chi_{N_j}:j\leq i\rangle\) is in the model \(N_{i+1}\) because the characteristic functions are definable from their associated model.
    The sequence \(\langle \chi_{N_i}:i<\kappa\rangle\) is increasing and continuous, with supremum \(\chi_N\text{.}\) Much of the work of this section will be to generalize Theorem [cross-reference to target(s) "thm2" missing or not unique] and show that there is a set \(F\subseteq\prod A\) of cardinality \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) such that \(\chi_N\in F\) for any \(\kappa\)-internally approachable \(N\) containing \(A\text{.}\) Even though these models might be quite arbitrary, there are relatively few possibilities for their characteristic functions.

    Subsection 2.3 Building generators from cofinality sequences

    Proposition 8. Suppose \(\lambda\in\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\cap N\text{,}\) and let \(\delta=\sup(N\cap\lambda)\text{.}\) If \(f\in\prod A\) is an upper bound for \(\langle f^\lambda_\alpha:\alpha<\delta\rangle\) mod \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} B_\lambda\subseteq_{J_{<\lambda}[A]}\{a\in A: \chi_N(a)\leq f(a)\}. \end{equation*}
    Proof. If this fails, then
    \begin{equation*} \{a\in B_\lambda: f(a)<\sup(N\cap a)\}\notin J_{<\lambda}[A]. \end{equation*}
    Since \(|A|\) is less than \(\kappa\text{,}\) we can find a single \(i<\kappa\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f(a)<\sup(N\cap a)\Longleftrightarrow f(a)<\sup(N_i\cap a), \end{equation*}
    and so
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn65.2} \{a\in B_\lambda: f(a)<\chi_{N_i}(a)\}\notin J_{<\lambda}[A]. \end{equation*}
    But the model \(N\) can see an \(\alpha<\lambda\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn65.3} \chi_{N_i}\restriction B_\lambda <_{J_{<\lambda}[A]} f^\lambda_\alpha\restriction B_\lambda. \end{equation*}
    In particular, there is such an \(\alpha\in N\cap\lambda\text{,}\) and putting ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn65.2" missing or not unique]) and ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn65.3" missing or not unique]) together tells us
    \begin{equation*} \{a\in B_\lambda: f(a)< f^\lambda_\alpha(a)\}\notin J_{<\lambda}[A]. \end{equation*}
    But this contradicts our assumption that \(f\) bounds \(\bar{f}^\lambda\restriction\delta\) mod \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{.}\) ◻
    This tells us that characteristic function \(\chi_N\) is a least upper bound for \(\langle f^\lambda_\alpha:\alpha<\sup(N\cap\lambda)\rangle\) modulo the ideal \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\restriction B_\lambda\text{.}\) But more is true – it is actually an exact upper bound.
    Corollary 9. Suppose \(\lambda\in N\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) and \(\delta = \sup(N\cap\lambda)\text{.}\) Then \(\chi_N\) is an exact upper bound for \(\langle f^\lambda_\alpha:\alpha<\delta\rangle\) modulo the ideal \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\restriction B_\lambda\text{,}\) and \(\delta\) is a flat point for the sequence \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) modulo the ideal \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\restriction B_\lambda\text{.}\)
    Proof. If \(g\) is below \(\chi_N\) modulo \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\restriction B_\lambda\text{,}\) then since \(|A|<\kappa\) there is a single \(i<\kappa\) such that \(\chi_{N_i}\) bounds \(g\) mod \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\restriction B_\lambda\text{.}\) But just as in the previous proof, \(N\) can see an ordinal \(\alpha<\lambda\) with
    \begin{equation*} \chi_{N_i}\restriction B_\lambda<_{J_{<\lambda}[A]} f^\lambda_\alpha\restriction B_\lambda, \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} (\exists \alpha<\delta)\left[ g < f^\lambda_\alpha\mod J_{<\lambda}[A]\restriction B_\lambda\right]. \end{equation*}
    To see that \(\delta\) is a flat point, we just note that
    \begin{equation*} \mathop{\mathrm{cf}}(\sup(N\cap a)) = \kappa \end{equation*}
    for each \(a\in A\text{,}\) witnessed by the strictly increasing sequence \(\langle \chi_{N_i}(a):i<\kappa\rangle\text{.}\) ◻
    We close this subsection with some more corollaries of Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop:CharGenProp" missing or not unique].
    Corollary 10. Suppose \(\lambda\in N\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{.}\) If \(\lambda<\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) and there is a \(g\in N\cap\prod A\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f\leq_{J_{\leq\lambda}[A]}g, \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} \{a\in A: \chi_N(a)\leq f(a)\}\text{ is a generator for }\lambda. \end{equation*}
    Proof. Because of Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop:CharGenProp" missing or not unique], we need only show that the set in question is in \(J_{\leq\lambda}[A]\text{.}\) Notice that \(g(a)<\chi_N(a)\) for all \(a\in A\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} \{a\in A: \chi_N(a)\leq f(a)\}\subseteq\{a\in A: g(a)<f(a)\}, \end{equation*}
    and this latter set is in \(J_{\leq\lambda}[A]\) by assumption. ◻
    Finally, we have the following:
    Corollary 11. If \(\lambda\in N\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) and \(\sup(N\cap\lambda)\leq\gamma<\lambda\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \{a\in A: \chi_N(a)\leq f^\lambda_\gamma(a)\}\text{ is a generator for }\lambda. \end{equation*}
    Proof. If \(\lambda = \max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) then this follows immediate from Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop:CharGenProp" missing or not unique]. If \(\lambda<\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) then \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) has an upper bound \(g\) modulo \(J_{\leq\lambda}[A]\) because \(\prod A/ J_{\leq\lambda}[A]\) is \(\lambda^+\)-directed. Now the previous corollary applies and we have the result. ◻

    Subsection 2.4 Minimal club continuity

    Definition 12. Let \(\bar{f}=\langle f^\lambda_\alpha:\alpha<\lambda\rangle\) be a cofinality sequence for \(\lambda\) in \(\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) and let \(\delta<\lambda\) be a limit ordinal of uncountable cofinality. We say that the sequence \(\bar{f}\) is minimally club continuous at \(\delta\) if for each \(a\in A\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn:65.1} f^\lambda_\delta(a) = \min\{\sup\{f^\lambda_\alpha(a): \alpha\in C\}: C\text{ closed unbounded in }\delta\}. \end{equation*}
    We say that \(\bar{f}\) is minimally club continuous at cofinality \(\kappa\) if the above holds for all \(\delta<\lambda\) of cofinality \(\kappa\text{.}\)
    Let us check that this definition is reasonable. Given \(\delta<\lambda\) of cofinality \(\kappa\) and \(a\in A\text{,}\) the right-hand side of ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.1" missing or not unique]) makes sense and so this equation defines a function in \(\prod A\text{.}\) If we let \(C(a)\) be a closed unbounded subset of \(\delta\) for which the minimizing value is obtained, then for any closed unbounded \(C\subseteq C(a)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f^\lambda_\delta(a) = \sup\{f^\lambda_\alpha(a):\alpha\in C\}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(|A|<\kappa\text{,}\) we can let \(C\) be the intersection of the sets \(C(a)\) for \(a\in A\text{,}\) and this gives us a single closed unbounded \(C\subseteq\delta\) with the property that for any closed unbounded \(D\subseteq C\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} (\forall a\in A)\left[f^\lambda_\delta(a) = \sup\{f^\lambda_\alpha(a):\alpha\in D\}\right]. \end{equation*}
    In particular, for each \(\alpha\in C\) we will have \(f_\alpha^\lambda\leq f^\lambda_\delta\text{,}\) and so by ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.1" missing or not unique]) we know that for any closed unbounded subset \(D\) of \(C\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} f^\lambda_\delta = \sup\{f^\lambda_\alpha:\alpha\in D\}. \end{equation*}
    In particulary, \(f^\lambda_\delta\) is an upper bound for \(\bar{f}^\lambda\restriction\delta\) modulo \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{.}\)
    Returning to our usual context, suppose that \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) is minimally club continuous at cofinality \(\kappa\text{,}\) and let \(N\) be a \(\kappa\)-internally approachable model containing \(\bar{f}_\lambda\text{.}\) What occurs at \(\delta = \sup(N\cap\lambda)\text{?}\)
    The function \(f^\lambda_\delta\) will not be in \(N\text{,}\) and neither is the minimizing club \(C\subseteq\delta\text{.}\) We do know, however, that \(C\) is a subset of \(N\) and this means that \(f^\lambda_\alpha\) is an element of \(N\) for every \(\alpha\in C\text{.}\) But then the range of \(f^\lambda_\alpha\) will be a subset of \(N\) (as \(A\subseteq N\)), so for each \(a\in A\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} f^\lambda_\delta(a) = \sup\{f^\lambda_\alpha(a):a\in C\}\leq\sup(N\cap a) = \chi_N(a), \end{equation*}
    and so
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn:65.8} f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}\leq \chi_N. \end{equation*}
    But we have already shown that \(\chi_N\restriction B_\lambda\) is an exact upper bound for \(\langle f_\alpha^\lambda\restriction B_\lambda:\alpha<\delta\rangle\) modulo \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{,}\) so it must be the case that
    \begin{equation*} \chi_N\restriction B_\lambda =_{J_{<\lambda}[A]} f^\lambda_\delta\restriction B_\lambda. \end{equation*}
    In the special case where \(\lambda\) is \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) we can assume \(B_\lambda = A\text{,}\) and we then have
    \begin{equation*} \chi_N =_{J_{<\lambda}[A]}f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}. \end{equation*}
    So reflecting back on our goal of trying to capture the characteristic functions of \(\kappa\)-internally approachable models, we have shown that if \(\lambda = \max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) and \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) is minimally club continuous at cofinality \(\kappa\text{,}\) then \(\chi_N\) will agree with \(f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}\) except perhaps on a set in \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{.}\) Our next move is to show that this set of exceptions, even though potentially not in \(N\text{,}\) can still be covered by a set in \(N\cap J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.5 Internal reflections

    Taking stock of our story so far, we let \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) and \(N\) be as usual, and define
    \begin{equation*} B^*_\lambda:=\{a\in A: \sup(N\cap a)\leq f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}(a)\}. \end{equation*}
    We know \(B^*_\lambda\) is a generator for \(\lambda\text{,}\) but since it is defined using objects that are external to \(N\text{,}\) we have no reason to believe that it will be in the model. In this subsection we will show that \(B^*_\lambda\) can be “reflected” inside the model \(N\text{,}\) and the existence of such reflections will be used as fuel for arguments by induction on \(\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{.}\)
    Given \(i<j<\kappa\text{,}\) let us define
    \begin{equation*} B^{i,j}_\lambda:=\{a\in A\cap(\lambda+1): \sup(N_i\cap a)< f^\lambda_{\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)}(a)\} \end{equation*}
    The set \(B^{i,j}_\lambda\) can be defined in the model \(N_{j+1}\text{,}\) so each \(B^{i,j}_\lambda\) is in \(N\text{.}\) We will show that for many choices of \(i\) and \(j\text{,}\) this set will also be a generator for \(\lambda\) that is a subset of \(B^*_\lambda\text{,}\) and this is how we produce our reflections of \(B^*_\lambda\) into \(N\text{.}\)
    Theorem 3. Under our usual assumptions, suppose \(\lambda\) is in \(N\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) while \(\bar{f}^\lambda\in N\) is minimally club continuous at cofinality \(\kappa\text{.}\) Then there is a closed unbounded \(E_\lambda\subseteq\kappa\) such that for each \(i<j\) in \(E_\lambda\) the set
    \begin{equation*} B^{i,j}_\lambda:=\{a\in A: \sup(N_i\cap a)< f^\lambda_{\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)}(a)\} \end{equation*}
    is a generator for \(\lambda\text{,}\) and
    \begin{equation*} B^{i,j}_\lambda\subseteq\{a\in A: \sup(N\cap a)\leq f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}(a)\}. \end{equation*}
    Proof. Before getting in to the proof, let us recall that \(B_\lambda\) is our “ambient” generator for \(\lambda\) that is an element of \(N\text{,}\) in contrast to \(B^*_\lambda\text{,}\) which is the generator we built by comparing \(f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}\) with \(\chi_N\text{.}\) Both of these will make an appearance in the argument.
    Let \(C\) be a closed unbounded subset of \(\kappa\) witnessing that \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) is minimally club continuous at \(N\cap\lambda\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn:65.6} (\forall a\in A)\left[f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}(a) = \sup\{f^\lambda_\alpha(a):\alpha\in C\}\right], \end{equation*}
    and this remains true for any closed unbounded subset of \(C\text{.}\)
    Next, since \(|A|<\kappa\text{,}\) we know for all sufficiently large \(\alpha<\kappa\) that
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn:65.4} f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}(a)< \sup(N\cap a) \Longleftrightarrow f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}(a)<\sup(N_\alpha\cap a). \end{equation*}
    Finally, given \(\beta<\kappa\text{,}\) there is an ordinal \(h(\beta)<\kappa\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn:65.5} h(\beta)\leq\gamma<\kappa\Longrightarrow \chi_{N_\beta}\restriction B_\lambda<_{J_{<\lambda}[A]} f^\lambda_\gamma\restriction B_\lambda \end{equation*}
    because \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) is a cofinality sequence for \(\lambda\text{.}\)
    We let \(E_\lambda\) be the closed unbounded subset of \(\kappa\) defined by putting \(\alpha\) into \(E_\lambda\) if
    • \(\alpha\) is large enough so that ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.4" missing or not unique]) holds,
    • \(\alpha\) is closed under the function \(h\) from ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.5" missing or not unique]), and
    • \(\sup(N_\alpha\cap\lambda)\in C\text{,}\) the closed unbounded set from ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.6" missing or not unique]).
    Note that \(E_\lambda\) is closed and unbounded in \(\kappa\) because the set \(\{\sup(N_\alpha\cap\lambda):\alpha<\kappa\}\) is too, so we now show that \(E_\lambda\) has the other required properties.
    Given \(i<j<\kappa\) in \(E_\lambda\text{,}\) suppose \(a\in B^{i,j}_\lambda\text{.}\) By definition, this means
    \begin{equation*} \sup(N_i\cap a)< f^\lambda_{\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)}(a). \end{equation*}
    But \(\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)\) is in \(C\text{,}\) so by ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.6" missing or not unique]),
    \begin{equation*} f^\lambda_{\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)}\leq f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}, \end{equation*}
    and therefore
    \begin{equation*} \sup(N_i\cap a)< f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}. \end{equation*}
    But now \(i\) is large enough so that by ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.4" missing or not unique]), we are allowed to conclude
    \begin{equation*} f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}(a) = \chi_N(a) \end{equation*}
    and hence \(a\in B^*_\lambda\text{.}\) Thus, \(B^{i, j}_\lambda\subseteq B^*_\lambda\text{.}\)
    To see that \(B^{i, j}_\lambda\) is a generator, we will need to use the fact that \(h(i)<j\) and \(j\leq\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)\text{.}\) This means
    \begin{equation*} \chi_{N_i}\restriction B_\lambda <_{J_{<\lambda}[A]} f^\lambda_{\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)}\restriction B_\lambda, \end{equation*}
    and so for \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\)-almost every \(a\in B_\lambda\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \sup(N_i\cap a)< f^\lambda_{\sup(N_j\cap\lambda)}(a). \end{equation*}
    In other words,
    \begin{equation*} B_\lambda\subseteq_{J_{<\lambda}[A]}B^{i, j}_\lambda, \end{equation*}
    and we are done. ◻
    This can be improved by using diagonal intersections to achieve a single closed unbounded \(E\subseteq\kappa\) that works for many \(\lambda\in N\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) simultaneously.
    Corollary 13. Suppose \(\langle \bar{f}^\lambda:\lambda\in\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\rangle\in N_0\) is such that each \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) is minimally club continuous at cofinality \(\kappa\text{.}\) There is a closed unbounded \(E\subseteq\kappa\) such that for any \(i<j<k\) in \(E\) and \(\lambda\in N_i\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) the set
    \begin{equation*} B^{j, k}_\lambda = \{a\in A: \sup(N_j\cap a)<f^\lambda_{\sup(N_k\cap\lambda)}(a)\} \end{equation*}
    is a generator for \(\lambda\text{,}\) and
    \begin{equation*} B^{j,k}_\lambda\subseteq\left\{a\in A:\sup(N\cap a)= f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}(a)\right\} \end{equation*}
    Proof. For \(\alpha<\kappa\text{,}\) we define
    \begin{equation*} E_\alpha:= \bigcap\{E_\lambda:\lambda\in N_\alpha\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\}. \end{equation*}
    This gives us a club subset of \(\kappa\) that “works” for each \(\lambda\) in \(N_\alpha\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{.}\) Now let \(E\) be the diagonal intersection of the \(E_\alpha\) for \(\alpha<\kappa\text{.}\) If \(i<j<k\) in \(E\) and \(\lambda\in N_i\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) then \(j\) and \(k\) are in \(E_\lambda\text{,}\) and we have what we need. ◻
    The strength of the preceding corollary is in the fact that for \(i<j<k\) in \(E\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \langle B^{j,k}_\lambda:\lambda\in N_i\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\rangle\in N_{j+1}, \end{equation*}
    so that \(N_{j+1}\) contains a sequence of generators for all \(\lambda\in N_i\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) that are each an internal reflection of the corresponding external generator \(B_\lambda^*\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.6 Characterizing characteristic functions

    We won’t need the full power of what we obtained in the previous subsection right now. What we actually use is the following: if \(\lambda\in\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\cap N\text{,}\) there is a set \(B'_\lambda\) such that
    • \(B'_\lambda\) is a generator for \(\lambda\text{,}\)
    • \(B'_\lambda\) is in \(N\text{,}\) and
    • \(f^\lambda_{\sup(N\cap\lambda)}\restriction B_\lambda = \chi_N\restriction B_\lambda\text{.}\)
    The model \(N\) sees that the first two of these statements are true, but the third statement is something external that someone inside of \(N\) will not be able to see. What makes the argument work is that if \(\lambda=\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) then \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A\setminus B'_\lambda)\) will be less than \(\lambda\) because \(B'_\lambda\) is a generator, and \(A\setminus B'_\lambda\) will still be in \(N\) because both \(A\) and \(B'_\lambda\) are. The exact definition of \(B'_\lambda\) won’t matter: we can just let \(i<j<k\) be consecutive members of \(E\) where \(i\) is least such that \(\lambda\in N_i\cap\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) and set
    \begin{equation*} B'_\lambda = B^{j, k}_\lambda \in N_{k+1}\subseteq N. \end{equation*}
    Theorem 4. Suppose \(N\) is a \(\kappa\)-internally approachable model containing \(A\text{,}\) and \(\bar{F}=\langle \bar{f}^\lambda:\lambda\in\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\rangle\in N\) is such that each \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) is minimally club continuous at cofinality \(\kappa\text{.}\) Then there are \(\lambda_0>\dots>\lambda_n\) in \(\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) with \(\lambda_0=\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} A = \bigcup_{i\leq n}B'_{\lambda_i} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \chi_N = \max\left\{f^{\lambda_0}_{\sup(N\cap\lambda_0)},\dots, f^{\lambda_n}_{\sup(N\cap\lambda_n)}\right\} \end{equation*}
    Proof. The proof consists of showing that \(A\) can be covered by finitely many of our reflected generators, and we will do this through a recursion that must terminate after finitely many steps. We start by setting \(\lambda_0\) equal to \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\text{,}\) and \(A_0 = A\text{.}\) At stage \(n\text{,}\) we will be handed a subset \(A_n\) of \(A\) that is in \(N\) and cardinals \(\lambda_i\) for \(i\leq n\text{.}\) We now ask the question
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn:65.7} \text{Is }A_n\setminus B'_{\lambda_n}=\emptyset? \end{equation*}
    If it is, then our construction terminates. If not, we define
    \begin{equation*} A_{n+1} = A_n\setminus B'_{\lambda_n} = A\setminus\bigcup_{i\leq n}B'_{\lambda_i}, \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_{n+1} = \max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A_{n+1}). \end{equation*}
    Notice that each \(A_i\) is in \(N\) because the generators \(B'_{\lambda_j}\) for \(j<i\) are available in \(N\text{,}\) so our construction can continue if needed. If \(A_{n+1}\) is defined, then
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_{n+1} &= \max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A_{n+1})\\ &=\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A_n\setminus B'_{\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A_n)})\\ &<\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A_n)=\lambda_n, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    so we cannot continue for infinitely many steps, and we must arrive eventually at a stage where the answer to ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.7" missing or not unique]) is “yes”. At that point, we know
    \begin{equation*} A = \bigcup_{i\leq n}B'_{\lambda_i}. \end{equation*}
    Now for each \(i\leq n\text{,}\) we know
    \begin{equation*} f^{\lambda_i}_{\sup(N\cap\lambda_i)}\restriction B_{\lambda_i}' = \chi_N\restriction B'_{\lambda_i} \end{equation*}
    by properties of \(B'_{\lambda_i}\text{,}\) and
    \begin{equation*} f^{\lambda_i}_{\sup(N\cap\lambda_i)}\leq \chi_N \end{equation*}
    by ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.8" missing or not unique]), so the result follows. ◻
    We state the following corollary with our assumptions spelled out explicitly.
    Corollary 14. Suppose \(A\) is a progressive set of regular cardinals with \(|A|^+<\min(A)\text{,}\) and \(\kappa\) is a regular cardinal such that \(|A|<\kappa<\min(A)\text{.}\) Then there is a set \(F\subseteq\prod A\) of cardinality \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)\) such that whenever \(N\) is a \(\kappa\)-internally approachable model with \(A\in N\text{,}\) the characteristic function of \(N\) is in \(F\text{.}\)
    This turns out to be a key ingredient in connecting pcf theory back to more traditional cardinal arithmetic. In particular, we want to show that in some situations, the characteristic function of \(N\) determines much of the structure of \(N\cap\mathop{\mathrm{Ord}}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.7 The cofinality of \(([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0},\subseteq)\)

    We will end this section with proving that the cofinality of the structure \(\left([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0},\subseteq\right)\) is \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(\{\aleph_n:n<\omega\})\text{.}\)
    Proposition 15. Suppose \(M\) and \(N\) are \(\aleph_n\)-internally approachable models for some non-zero \(n<\omega\text{,}\) and for all \(k<\omega\)
    \begin{equation*} \sup(N\cap\aleph_k) = \sup(M\cap\aleph_k). \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{equation*} M\cap\aleph_\omega = N\cap\aleph_\omega. \end{equation*}
    Proof. We show by induction on \(k<\omega\) that \(M\cap\aleph_k = N\cap\aleph_k\text{.}\) This is true for \(k\leq n\) because \(\omega_n+1\subseteq M\cap N\text{,}\) so assume \(M\cap\aleph_k = N\cap\aleph_k\) for some \(k\geq n\text{,}\) and let
    \begin{equation*} \delta = \sup(M\cap\aleph_{k+1}) = \sup(N\cap\aleph_{k+1}). \end{equation*}
    Since \(M\) and \(N\) are both \(\aleph_n\)-internally approachable, we know that
    \begin{equation*} \mathop{\mathrm{cf}}(\delta)=\aleph_n \end{equation*}
    and there is a closed unbounded \(C\subseteq\aleph_n\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \alpha\in C\Longrightarrow \sup(M_\alpha\cap\aleph_{k+1}) = \sup(N_\alpha\cap\aleph_{k+1}). \end{equation*}
    If we define
    \begin{equation*} D:=\{\sup(M_\alpha\cap\aleph_{k+1}):\alpha\in C\}=\{\sup(N_\alpha\cap\aleph_{k+1}):\alpha\in C\} \end{equation*}
    then \(D\) is a subset of \(M\cap N\) that is closed and unbounded in \(\delta\text{.}\)
    It suffices to prove that \(M\cap\aleph_{k+1}\) is subset of \(N\cap\aleph_{k+1}\text{.}\) Given \(\alpha\in M\cap\aleph_{k+1}\) we can fix an ordinal \(\gamma\in D\) such that \(\alpha\leq\gamma\text{.}\) This ordinal \(\gamma\) is in both \(M\) and \(N\) and of cardinality at most \(\aleph_k\text{,}\) so that there is a function \(f\) in \(M\cap N\) mapping \(\aleph_k\) onto \(\gamma\text{.}\) The ordinal \(\beta=f^{-1}(\{\alpha\})\) is an element of \(M\cap \aleph_k\text{,}\) hence an element of \(N\cap\aleph_k\) by our induction hypothesis. But now
    \begin{equation*} \alpha = f(\beta) \in N \end{equation*}
    and we are done. ◻
    The above proof admits many generalizations, and we shall take a look at some of these later on. The important thing to remember is that models are tightly connected with their characteristic functions, and in some sense this is why pcf theory has things to say about cardinal arithmetic: we can interchange information between sets of ordinals and products of functions using elementary submodels and their characteristic functions. But for now, we will be content with the following theorem which was promised in the introduction of this section.
    Theorem 5. \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(\{\aleph_n:n<\omega\}) = \mathop{\mathrm{cf}}\left([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0},\subseteq\right)\text{.}\)
    Proof. Let \(\lambda = \max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(\{\aleph_n:n<\omega\})\text{,}\) let \(\mu\) denote the cofinality of \([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0}\text{,}\) and define
    \begin{equation*} A:=\{\aleph_n:2\leq n<\omega\}. \end{equation*}
    We note that \(\max\mathop{\mathrm{pcf}}(A)=\lambda\) as well.
    Any countable subset \(\aleph_\omega\) is covered by a set of the form
    \begin{equation*} \label{eqn:65.9} M\cap\aleph_\omega \end{equation*}
    for some \(\aleph_1\)-internally approachable model \(M\text{.}\) There are at most \(\lambda\) possibilities for the characteristic function of \(M\) in \(A\) by Corollary [cross-reference to target(s) "cor:CaptureChar" missing or not unique], and so there at most \(\lambda\) sets of the form ([cross-reference to target(s) "eqn:65.9" missing or not unique]) by Proposition [cross-reference to target(s) "prop:ModEquProp" missing or not unique]. So let us define
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{X} = \{M\cap\aleph_{\omega}:\text{ $M$ is an $\aleph_1$-approachable model containing $A$}\}. \end{equation*}
    We know \(|\mathcal{X}|\leq\lambda\text{,}\) and any countable subset of \(\aleph_\omega\) is covered by a set in \(\mathcal{X}\text{.}\)
    To finish, note that if \(|X|=\aleph_1\) then the cofinality of \([X]^{\aleph_0}\) is \(\aleph_1\text{.}\) So if \(\mathcal{P}_X\) is a cofinal subset of \([X]^{\aleph_0}\) of cardinality \(\aleph_1\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{P} = \bigcup\{\mathcal{P}_X: X\in\mathcal{X}\} \end{equation*}
    is a cofinal subset of \([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0}\) of cardinality at most \(\lambda\text{,}\) and \(\mu\leq\lambda\text{.}\)
    For the other direction, assume \(\mu\) is strictly less than \(\lambda\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{P}\) be a cofinal subset of \([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0}\) of cardinality \(\mu\text{.}\) Let \(\bar{f}^\lambda=\langle f^\lambda_\alpha:\alpha<\lambda\rangle\) be a scale in \(\prod A\) modulo \(J_{<\lambda}[A]\text{,}\) and for each \(\alpha<\lambda\) let \(X_\alpha\) be the range of \(f^\lambda_\alpha\text{.}\)
    Now \(\mathcal{P}\) is cofinal in \([\aleph_\omega]^{\aleph_0}\) so for each \(\alpha<\lambda\) there is an \(Y\in\mathcal{P}\) with \(X_\alpha\subseteq Y\text{.}\) Since \(\lambda\) is a regular cardinal greater than \(|\mathcal{P}|\text{,}\) there must exist a single \(Y\in\mathcal{P}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} |\{\alpha<\lambda:\mathop{\mathrm{ran}}(f^\lambda_\alpha)\subseteq Y\}|=\lambda. \end{equation*}
    Now let us consider the function \(g\in\prod A\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} g(\aleph_n) = \sup(Y\cap\aleph_n)+1. \end{equation*}
    Since \(\bar{f}^\lambda\) is a cofinality sequence for \(\lambda\text{,}\) we know \(g\leq_{J_{<\lambda}}f^\lambda_\alpha\) for all sufficiently large \(\alpha<\lambda\text{.}\) But this cannot be the case if \(\alpha\) is such that \(\mathop{\mathrm{ran}}(f^\lambda_\alpha)\subseteq Y\text{,}\) and we have a contradiction. ◻
